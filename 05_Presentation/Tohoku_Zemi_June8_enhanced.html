Observed promotion timelines

Observed completion times look similar in some parts of the pipeline, but they already reflect who appears in the data and who reaches visible milestones.
Who appears

Women remain a minority in the full PhD-holder cohort and in both observed transition samples.

How to read the figure

Among completed transitions, the distributions overlap strongly by gender, but median timelines still vary by field and stage; these medians do not capture the full promotion process.

Shorter or similar observed timelines among promoted women do not automatically imply equality; they need to be read together with representation and selection.
Figure 1 has three parts. Panel a shows the gender composition of the total PhD-holder sample and the two transition samples. Women account for a smaller share throughout the pipeline. Panel b shows the distribution of years to promotion for Stage 1 and Stage 2. The male and female distributions overlap, and among completed transitions women do not always look slower. Panel c breaks median years to promotion down by field and stage. The key point I want to make is that observed promotion time is not the same as full career opportunity. These figures describe the people whose promotion milestones are visible in researchmap, so I avoid saying that women are advantaged. Instead, I treat this as evidence that promotion timing must be interpreted together with selection and representation.

Figure 2 moves from overall description to cohort dynamics. Panels a and b compare median promotion years by cohort for Stage 1 and Stage 2. The Stage 1 pattern is more informative for my next study because earlier cohorts show clearer female delays, while later cohorts move closer to parity but are also more affected by limited observation time. Panel c shows that women’s share increases across cohorts, but this does not mean the pipeline is fully equal. Panel d is especially important because it shows the realization rate. For recent cohorts, many researchers have not yet had enough time to complete promotion, so we have to be careful with simple completed-transition comparisons. This is why the next analysis will focus on PhD to Associate Professor and will use censoring-aware or person-year models.

Reviewer feedback turned a timeline study into a mechanism agenda

1. Observability

Researchmap completeness is non-random; analyses must distinguish full PhD cohort, observed transitions, and positive-duration samples.

2. Productivity

Adding stage-specific papers and projects attenuates some later-cohort female effects, especially in Stage 1 STEM.

3. Right-censoring

Cox models are more conservative. Stage 1 remains central, but completed-transition OLS can overstate female advantage.

These concerns point to the same next question:
What pre-promotion resources and networks shape early-career promotion?
So the next studies are not separate extensions. They follow directly from the limits of a timeline-only analysis.

Study 2: Pre-promotion academic capital and PhD→AP promotion

Do similar KAKEN funding histories and network positions help women and men move from PhD to AP at the same rate?
Outcome

Yearly risk of first AP appointment among researchers after PhD and before AP or censoring.

Key predictors

KAKEN access, PI/Co-I status, direct funding amount, project tier, collaboration network position.

Model logic

Person-year panel; use t−1 or rolling-window variables to predict promotion in year t.

Bias to avoidSolution
Longer time to AP gives more years to accumulate KAKEN funding.Use lagged yearly variables and 3/4/5-year rolling windows, not raw pre-AP totals as the main predictor.
In Study 2, the goal is to explain early-career promotion using time-ordered funding and network measures.
Stress the exposure-time problem. Say “given what the researcher had before this year, did they get promoted this year?”

Dynamic KAKEN networks capture access to leadership, information, and recognition

Important coding rule

PI / Co-I is a project-specific role, not a permanent node attribute. The same researcher may be PI in one project and Co-I in another.

Why the network matters

The network captures access to collaboration opportunities, project leadership, elite university ties, and cross-field bridges before promotion.

So the KAKEN network is not just a descriptive graph. It is one way to measure pre-promotion academic capital.
Explain R1 and R2 may have multiple collaborations in the same year. Mention PI-to-Co-I directed network as an extension.

Study 3 asks what changes after promotion to Associate Professor

Promotion is not only an outcome; it may also change access to later resources.
Design
  • Sample: researchers who reached AP
  • Event-time panel: year − AP_year
  • Window: AP−5 to AP+5, with flexibility depending on data quality
Outcomes

Post-AP KAKEN access, PI probability, direct funding, network centrality, and publications if reliable.

What this adds

The project connects promotion timing, academic capital, and post-promotion accumulation within one longitudinal framework.

Discussion questions
  • Is PhD→AP the right main transition for Study 2?
  • Should funding and network indicators be modeled jointly?
  • Which post-AP outcome should be prioritized?
  • How should incomplete publication records be treated?
The broader aim is to move beyond rank representation and ask how academic career advantages are formed and then carried forward over time.
